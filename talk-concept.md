# Talk Concept

Conventional wisdom will tell the budding software engineer that if they want to find a good job, they should invest in the most popular technologies. But is it true?

What I know is true is that you have to start somewhere, but that starting place doesn't have to be where you end up.


## Countering Conventional Wisdom

A healthy counter-point to conventional wisdom.



### Business Application Life Cycle

Let me tell you what it's like to professionally build business software.

- You're excited, you get to build something new. You're going to do a great job. You've learned a lot and you're excited that you're going to make a big impact on the business.
- You build the software, things are going great, you're responding quickly, people are excited about how fast things are moving.
- Time passes, some assumptions you made about how things work were wrong. That's normal, that's good. That's what it feels like to mature a project.
- Time passes, the project is full of features and flows that have to be maintained. Feature requests come in and because there's already so much functionality in the application, you have to be careful about how you build things because it can affect other features.
- Time passes, it's becoming difficult to work in the code. It use to be that when you needed to add something new or change something, you could identify what the new system would need to become and change the whole system.. but now it's just too much. so you sort of identify a way to get what you need without affecting other things too much and implement that.
- Time passes, your deployments are more and more likely to create operational incidents in production. Your manager is frustrated with how long it takes to implement new features. You're frustrated at the legacy code base, it's frustrating to work in.. it feels bad to deploy "lemons".
- You and your colleagues keep requesting the budget for a rewrite of parts of the system or maybe even the whole system. The business is not pumped because rewrites are expensive and delay the release of new functionality.
- You pressure and pressure, management is frustrated and decides that it has become necessary, and you are permitted the budget.
- You're excited, you get to build something new, you're going to do a great job. You've learned a lot and you're excited that you're going to make a big impacct on the business.

And the cycle starts over. I have lived through this process many many times and I've seen it at companies where I was consulting. This is a recurring pattern.

But let's step back for a second and think about how it FEELS. What is it LIKE to be in this process.

#### The experience of the engineer.

As an engineer you want to do a good job and honestly you have to know a LOT to be an effective software engineer. You're certain that you're going to make the right thing and do a good job.

Then over time things get sour. You're responsible for production errors. Your manager is frustrated that you're not moving faster. You feel like you're not experiencing the autonomy and respect that someone who has to have learned so much and who works so hard and who tries to do such a good job deserves.

Then look at the manager's perspective.

#### The experience of the manager.

Your engineers are slowing down, often you have to focus them.. you have to prioritize and determine what's important for the business because so little is getting done. We have business objectives to meet after all.. and shit flows down hill.. when the CEO's strategic goals are working out, everyone is happy. But when the software part, the part that this person is responsible for managing is holding things up.. well things aren't fun.

Why are the developers messing up so much lately? Why are they slow? Why do they keep trying to focus on technical things instead of product things?

Because the managers are put into a bad situation and because the engineers are supposed to be responsible for the technical delivery and technical excellence they begin to realize that they may need to micro manage the engineers.

####

So now the management is too involved.. the engineers don't feel the autonomy. The engineers blame the managers for not giving them the prioritization to fix what's broken. The managers feel like they have to treat the engineers like children who can't see the big picture. This is not a healthy situation.

And here's the thing. Management makes a lot of mistakes. They tend to not really know what they're doing. But when it comes to how the software works, the engineers are to blame.

#### What is the responsibility of the engineer?

If you think that the engineer's job is to make a new capability work, or to change an old capability. You're at best only partially right. Being able to make something work is a day 1 requirement. You come into the job with some ability to program and to build something that can work.

There is a lot more that a software engineer has to excel at in order to serve their function. Delivering functionality is small picture. Delivering the right functionality in the right way requires big picture thinking. Where is the business heading? What is it that this feature is seeking to solve? What is coming next after this? How can we know what trade-offs to make today and what to START thinking about for tomorrow?

But first and foremost I want to focus on this loop of disappointment, this cycle of shame and infantilism. What went wrong here?

The problem, over and over, every time. Is that when the system must change, developers have two paths in front of them:

1. identify how the system must change for the new feature and change the entire system so that it's fully aligned with its new way of being
2. don't change how the system functions in a significant way, instead identify a quick route to deliver the feature and deliver it quickly

<!--insert concrete examples--/>

And here's the thing.. software engineers know that they shouldn't use shortcuts.. but they are kind of forced into it.. For one, they have a bunch of pressure from management.. after all.. the ceo's big strategic initiative is really important. But also.. the system has so many features that in order to modify the system into the form it SHOULD be in, the same kind of form it would be in if we performed a complete rewrite, we'd have to modify 15 features all at once. And that's basically impossible. Not only do we not have the budget, but it'd take a giga brain to figure out how to navigate all of that. So we don't. We implement the shortcut, deliver what was asked and move on. Maybe we even deliver it so fast that we're considered a hero.

But all this does is accumulate debt.. more and more debt.. until the inevitable happens.. things get so bad.. and we have to start over so that we can build a system that is what it needs to be, not a pile of hacks that we put into place.
